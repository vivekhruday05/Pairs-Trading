Core Hypothesis

Why Causality Over Correlation?

The central research question

Hypothesis: Incorporating Granger causality into the pairs selection pipeline significantly enhances out-of-sample robustness and risk-adjusted returns compared to Pearson correlation alone.

Pairs trading rests on the premise that two assets sharing similar fundamental drivers maintain a stable long-term relationship. When microstructure noise or liquidity shocks force divergence, the arbitrageur shorts the outperformer and buys the underperformer—expecting mean reversion.

While correlation identifies co-movement, it fails to capture structural, information-driven dependencies. Two random walks with drift can exhibit high correlation yet diverge permanently.

Section 2.1 — Stochastic Processes

Asset Price Dynamics

Geometric Brownian Motion & I(1) processes

Asset prices are modeled as Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM):

dPi,t = μᵢ Pᵢ,t dt + σᵢ Pᵢ,t dWᵢ,t

Taking log-prices pi,t = ln(Pi,t), we obtain arithmetic Brownian motion:

dpi,t = (μᵢ − ½σᵢ²) dt + σᵢ dWᵢ,t
Key implication: Individual log-prices are non-stationary — integrated of order one, I(1). Classical regression on these yields spurious regressions. We must find a stationary linear combination.
Drift Parameter μᵢ

Expected instantaneous return. Under risk-neutral measure Q, μᵢ = r (risk-free rate) for derivative pricing. Under P, it captures the equity risk premium.

Volatility Parameter σᵢ

Governs the quadratic variation of the process. For pairs trading, σᵢ determines position sizing via the inverse-volatility scaler kt.

Wiener Process Wᵢ,t

Standard Brownian motion: W(0) = 0, increments W(t)−W(s) ∼ N(0, t−s), independent for non-overlapping intervals.

Section 2.2 — Cointegration

Engle-Granger Two-Step Method

Testing for a stationary spread

If a linear combination [1, −β]ᵀ of two I(1) series produces an I(0) process, they are cointegrated. The spread is defined as:

Sₜ = py,t − β · px,t
1
Cointegrating Regression (OLS)

Estimate py,t = α + β px,t + εₜ via OLS. The estimator β̂ is super-consistent under cointegration — converges at rate T rather than √T.

β̂ = (XᵀX)⁻¹ Xᵀy
2
ADF Test on Residuals

Test ε̂ₜ for stationarity via Augmented Dickey-Fuller regression. Reject H₀: γ = 0 (unit root) at 5% to confirm cointegration.

Δε̂ₜ = γ ε̂ₜ₋₁ + Σᵢ δᵢ Δε̂ₜ₋ᵢ + uₜ

H₀: γ = 0 (non-stationary) → reject if ADF stat < critical value
Hedge Ratio β

Determines the dollar-weighting between the two legs. A stationary β is critical — drift in β signals structural regime change in the cointegrating relationship.

ADF Critical Values

For residual-based tests (MacKinnon 1994): 1% ≈ −3.90, 5% ≈ −3.34, 10% ≈ −3.05. Stricter than standard DF due to estimation uncertainty in β̂.

Implementation: statsmodels.tsa.stattools.coint() executes both steps, returning the ADF statistic and p-value for the residuals.

Section 2.3 — Spread Dynamics

Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Process

Continuous-time mean-reverting model for the spread

A stationary spread Sₜ is modeled as an OU process — the continuous-time analog of a discrete AR(1):

dSₜ = θ(μ − Sₜ) dt + σ dWₜ

The conditional expectation demonstrates exponential decay toward equilibrium:

𝔼[Sₜ | S₀] = S₀ e^{−θt} + μ(1 − e^{−θt})

The half-life of mean reversion — the expected time for deviation to halve:

τ = ln(2) / θ

In discrete time (Δt = 1 day): ΔSₜ = λSₜ₋₁ + eₜ, so θ ≈ −λ and τ = −ln(2)/λ

θ — Mean Reversion Speed

θ > 0 required for stationarity. Large θ → rapid reversion → short holding periods ideal. Estimated from AR(1) regression on the spread.

μ — Long-run Equilibrium

The attractor of the spread. Trading signals are generated relative to deviations from μ, normalized by σ to form the Z-score.

σ — Spread Volatility

Governs the stationary variance of the spread: Var(Sₜ) = σ²/2θ. Lower σ and higher θ produce tighter, more tradeable spreads.

Practical implication: The half-life τ directly informs our max holding period Tmax = 30 days, since most pairs exhibit τ between 10–40 days.

Section 2.4 — Causality

Granger Causality vs. Pearson Correlation

Filtering structural relationships from spurious co-movements

Pearson correlation measures linear co-movement but cannot distinguish structural from spurious relationships:

ρX,Y = Cov(X,Y) / (σX σY)

Granger Causality: X Granger-causes Y if past values of X improve forecasts of Y beyond Y's own history. Tested via VAR:

Yₜ = a₀ + Σⱼ aⱼYₜ₋ⱼ + Σⱼ bⱼXₜ₋ⱼ + eₜ

H₀: b₁ = b₂ = ... = bₘ = 0
(X does not Granger-cause Y)

F-test rejects H₀ if past X has predictive power for Y. We require p-value < 0.05 in at least one direction.

Key insight: Many pairs with ρ > 0.8 exhibit Granger p-values above 0.4 — indicating no predictive causality despite high historical correlation.
Selection Criterion

Pairs must satisfy: (1) correlation ≥ threshold, (2) ADF p-value ≤ 0.05 on residuals, (3) minimum Granger p-value < 0.05 across bidirectional tests.

Why Correlation Fails Out-of-Sample

Exogenous shocks affect correlated but structurally unrelated assets differently, destroying the correlation without ever triggering reversion. Causal pairs contain an information flow anchor.

Implementation

statsmodels.tsa.stattools.grangercausalitytests() executes the VAR-based F-test. Minimum p-value across bidirectional tests is stored in PairAnalysisReport.

08 / 15 MA8.402 · Signal Generation
Section 3.1 & 3.2 — Trading Signals

Z-Score Signals & Inverse Volatility Sizing

Dynamic signal generation and risk-adjusted position sizing

Rolling mean and standard deviation over window W:

μS,t = (1/W) Σᵢ Sₜ₋ᵢ

σS,t = √[(1/(W−1)) Σᵢ (Sₜ₋ᵢ − μS,t)²]

Dynamic Z-score and trading logic:

Zₜ = (Sₜ − μS,t) / σS,t

Short spread: Zₜ ≥ Zentry
Long spread: Zₜ ≤ −Zentry
Exit: |Zₜ| ≤ Zexit

Inverse volatility position sizing targets constant gross exposure:

kₜ = Etarget / max(σΔS,t, σmin)

wy,t = sgn(Zₜ) · kₜ / (1 + |β|)
wx,t = −sgn(Zₜ) · β · kₜ / (1 + |β|)
Risk invariant: Gross exposure Gₜ = |wy,t| + |wx,t| is always capped at Gmax = 2.0, preventing leverage blowup during low-volatility regimes.
PnL Calculation

Daily mark-to-market PnL = Σᵢ wᵢ,ₜ · (Pᵢ,ₜ − Pᵢ,ₜ₋₁) / Pᵢ,ₜ₋₁. Transaction costs deducted as TC_rate × |Δwᵢ,ₜ|.

Section 3.3 — Risk Overlays

Three-Layer Risk Management

Stop-loss · Time-stop · VaR overlay

Stop Loss

If cumulative PnL of an active trade falls below −5% of allocated capital, position is instantly liquidated.

Trigger: PnLcum ≤ −SL_fraction × Capital

Tight stop (<3%) chokes mean reversion before it occurs. Optimal: ~5% (Insight 5).

Time Stop

Positions held longer than Tmax = 30 days are forcibly closed, assuming cointegration breakdown.

Trigger: days_held > Tmax = 30

Calibrated to median half-life τ across the universe (10–40 days). Validated by Insight 9.

VaR Overlay

Rolling 95% historical VaR is computed. If breached, position sizes are proportionally reduced.

VaR95% = μPnL − 1.645 σPnL
(scipy.stats.norm.ppf)

Ensures portfolio tail risk remains within institutional bounds during volatility spikes.

Architecture: All signals from PairSignalEngine are intercepted by PairRiskEngine before reaching PairBacktestEngine. Risk overrides are logged separately to enable attribution analysis between alpha decay and risk-triggered exits.

Section 4 — Software Architecture

Modular Python Engine Pipeline

Strictly decoupled object-oriented architecture

①
DataDownloader — Data Ingestion

Fetches adjusted close prices via yfinance API concurrently. Implements local filesystem caching via joblib to avoid redundant network calls during repeated backtesting runs.

②
PairIdentificationEngine — Pair Selection

Computes O(N²) pairwise correlations → Engle-Granger cointegration test → VAR Granger causality tests. Outputs PairAnalysisReport: {β, τ, ADF p-value, Granger p-value}.

③
PairSignalEngine — Signal Generation

State machine implements Z-score thresholding (+1 long, −1 short, 0 flat). Computes inverse-volatility scale kₜ, assigns normalized dollar weights, clips to max_gross_exposure.

④
PairRiskEngine — Risk Enforcement

Intercepts raw signals chronologically, applies stop-loss, time-stop, and VaR overlay. Overrides signal to 0 when any limit is breached. Operates as a stateful iterator.

⑤
PairBacktestEngine — Accounting Ledger

Simulates daily mark-to-market mechanics. Deducts transaction costs (TC_rate × turnover). Returns BacktestResult with equity curve, Sharpe Ratio, and Maximum Drawdown utilities.

Sensitivity Analyses: Insights 1–5

Performance, drawdown, returns, transaction costs, stop-loss

Insight 01 — Equity Curve

Causal PnL climbs steadily; Correlation PnL suffers a massive mid-2023 dip. Exogenous shocks that affect only one leg destroy correlation-based pairs without triggering reversion.

Insight 02 — Drawdown

Correlation approach spends vast majority of the out-of-sample period in deep drawdown (−7.80%). Causal approach experiences milder, short-lived drawdowns due to robust mean-reverting spreads.

Insight 03 — Return KDE

Causal daily returns: tight distribution centered slightly right of zero — consistent small gains. Correlation returns: left-skewed, flatter — greater volatility and frequent substantial losses.

Insight 04 — TC Sensitivity

Sweeping 0–50 bps: Causal approach remains profitable up to ~30 bps. Correlation approach is unviable across the entire spectrum. Causal pairs generate sufficient gross alpha.

Insight 05 — Stop-Loss Sweep

Tight SL (<3%) chokes mean reversion. Optimal at ~5%. For correlation pairs, widening SL simply leads to deeper terminal losses — the spread never reverted.

Section 5.4 — 10 Experiments (Part 2)

Sensitivity Analyses: Insights 6–10

Half-life, causality scatter, hedge ratio stability, holding period, exposure

Insight 06 — Half-Life Distribution

Most cointegrated pairs exhibit τ between 10–40 days. Mathematically justifies Tmax = 30 days. Holding beyond τ introduces uncompensated drift risk.

Insight 07 — Corr vs Granger

High ρ does not guarantee low Granger p-value. Many pairs with ρ > 0.8 have p-values above 0.4. Selecting from the bottom-right quadrant (high corr, low p-val) is the key to superior performance.

Insight 08 — Rolling β Stability

60-day rolling hedge ratio β for causal pairs exhibits mean-reverting stationarity without structural regime shifts — confirming the cointegrating vector is stable over time.

Insight 09 — Holding Period

Sweeping 5–60 days: returns maximize around 30 days, perfectly aligning with median half-life. Too short (5–10 days) cuts trades before mean reversion completes.

Insight 10 — Gross Exposure

Despite inverse-volatility sizing scaling up in quiet regimes, realized gross exposure strictly obeys Gmax = 2.0 cap throughout — proving the invariant bound of our capital allocation algorithm.

Section 6 — Future Extensions

MLE, Kelly Criterion & Market Impact

Three theoretical extensions for production-grade deployment

MLE of OU Process

Euler-Maruyama discretization yields an exact AR(1). Log-likelihood:

ℒ(a,b,σ²) = −n/2 ln(2π) −n/2 ln(σ²) − Σ(Sᵢ − a − bSᵢ₋₁)²/2σ²
θ = −ln(b̂)/Δt
μ = â/(1−b̂)
σ = σ̂η √(2θ/(1−b̂²))

Enables dynamic linking of Tmax to 2τ or 3τ per pair.

Fractional Kelly Sizing

Optimal leverage to maximize long-run growth rate for Gaussian processes:

f* = μexcess / σ²portfolio

For OU-driven pairs, instantaneous expected drift = θ|Sₜ − μ|:

f*ₜ = θ|Sₜ − μ| / σ²

Fractional: ffrac = c · f*ₜ, c ∈ (0, 0.5]

Theoretically amplifies Sharpe but increases max drawdown volatility.

Almgren-Chriss Impact

Linear cost model inadequate for large AUM. Square-root market impact (Almgren-Chriss):

TCtotal = cfixed·V + γ σdaily V √(V/ADV)

For current $100K backtest vs. UNH/PEP ADV >$1B: slippage term negligible. Critical above $50M AUM — defines strategy capacity bounds.

Future work: Transfer Entropy or deep causal discovery for non-linear dependencies; multi-asset Johansen basket arbitrage.

Section 8 — Summary

Conclusion & Future Directions

Causal inference as the cornerstone of robust statistical arbitrage

Core finding: Simplistic correlation screens produce pairs that catastrophically disintegrate out-of-sample. Spurious relationships identified in-sample led to a −7.80% drawdown and −0.738 Sharpe Ratio.

Causal approach: Granger causality acts as a powerful structural filter, isolating information-driven pairs. Out-of-sample Sharpe of +0.516 with only −5.74% drawdown under full risk management.

Risk architecture: The three-layer risk engine (stop-loss + time-stop + VaR) proved essential — particularly the time-stop calibrated to the OU half-life τ, and the Gmax = 2.0 exposure invariant.

Transaction cost robustness: Causal pairs generate sufficient gross alpha to remain profitable up to ~30 bps per trade — within institutional execution friction bounds.
